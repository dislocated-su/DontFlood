Hydropower project

Managing impacts

Hydropower, if improperly planned, can cause a severe damage to the environment. In this page, we discuss how we tackle environmental issues our project could cause.

Source: www.renewableenergyhub.com

Whilst smaller plants that use run-of-the-river systems may have a lesser impact, there still needs to be an assesment done on how that will affect the course of the river and what impact it may have on river life.


Problems we encountered


Endangered frog species

During our research, we found out that there is an endangered species of frog that lives on banks of the river, which habitats are going to be destroyed during construction. Now we understand the importance of saving endangered species, so we are planning to build a habitat for them and carefully move the frogs using local human resources. During the constructions, the frogs will temporarily live in those habitats, and then will be released back to their safe origin area.

This solution should keep the frogs from going extinct, but will require additional human resources.

Farming

We have predicted that farmers may lose arable farmland due to the route of the tunnel. To tackle this issue, we came up with a win-win solution. We adjusted the route of our tunnel to bore through a mountain, so that farmers do not lose land, and we can sell materials acquired during the process of boring.



Local power

Now, although some local residents are not happy about a hydroelectric power stations being built right next to them, without supplying electricity to them. To combat this challenge, we decided to connect them to the national grid while connecting the turbine so that they could access the power from the national grid on the standard rates.


Additional considerations

A major concern about using hydropower is blockage of fish migration. Our team had that in mind when desinging the dam, and we came up with a solution.

The Salmon Cannon

While there are other ways of transporting fish upstream through a dam, this one seems most efficient at the given time. Name describes the concept pretty well.

Source: www.npr.org

The device uses a pressure differential to suck up a fish, send it through a tube at up to 22 mph and then shoot it out the other side, reaching heights of up to 30 feet. This weekend, it will be used to move hatchery fish up a tributary of the Columbia River in Washington.
